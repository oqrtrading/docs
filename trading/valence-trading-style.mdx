## Markets without historical dependencies

Prediction markets are **event-driven**. A contract resolves when something happens (or doesn’t)—an election, a game result, a rate decision. There’s no long-lived asset with decades of price history. There’s no recurring “same name” that trades every day so that yesterday’s chart “matters” in the same way it might in equities.

That **independence from history** is central. Past prices in a prediction market are just **past beliefs**. They don’t form a causal structure that repeats. New information can rewrite the whole distribution in hours or minutes. So the right mental model is: *what’s the current information set, and where is the market now?*—not *what did the chart do last time?*

## Why technical analysis is the wrong tool

Imagine using classic technical analysis here. Support and resistance, moving-average crosses, head-and-shoulders—those ideas were built for markets where the same underlying trades over long periods and where some historical dependencies might (sometimes) persist. In prediction markets:

- The **event hasn’t happened yet**. There’s no “same asset” with decades of history. There’s one resolution.
- **Regime shifts** are the norm. A single headline can move the whole curve. “Historical patterns” are often from a different information regime and have little predictive power.
- **Liquidity and structure** change fast. Thin books, binary payoffs, and event resolution don’t match the assumptions behind most technical frameworks.

So relying on technical analysis as your primary edge would be a mistake. Chart patterns tell you where people used to disagree; they don’t tell you how *new* information will be priced. Your edge in prediction markets comes from **interpreting new information and current flow**, not from replaying historical price patterns.

That doesn’t mean you ignore the chart. Charts can help with orientation—where we’ve been, how volatile the market has been. But they’re secondary. Don’t let a technical signal override live order flow and fresh information.

## What to focus on instead

- **Current information** — News, events, and the current consensus. That’s what moves probability.
- **Current flow and liquidity** — Who’s trading, at what size, and how fast price is repricing. Your **blotter** and the live tape show execution quality and where the market is *now*, not where it was last week.
- **Fair and edge** — Your valuation (fair) and your reason to trade (edge). See [Fair values](/trading/fair-values) and the [Trading tutorial](/trading-tutorial/introduction).
- **Consolidated position and risk** — Net exposure in one unit so you know when you’re flat and when you’re taking risk. See [Your consolidated position](/trading-tutorial/consolidated-position).

Your blotter is still a core tool: it shows fills, slippage, and how quickly your orders get hit or lifted. Use it to monitor execution quality and to see how new information is getting priced in real time. But the broader point is that prediction markets reward **information and flow**, not historical pattern-fitting. Trade with that independence in mind.
