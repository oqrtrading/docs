## Start with the trade blotter

Your trade blotter is your primary decision tool.
It shows your exact fills, sizes, timestamps, and prices.
It also shows how fast the market is actually trading.

In most prediction markets, the tape matters more than static chart patterns.
You are trading event-driven probability updates, not long-lived cash-flow trends.
That means current flow usually carries more information than old prints.

## Why the trade tape matters more than long history

Historical data often has lower signal in prediction markets than in equities.
Many markets are short-dated and resolve on a single event.
Regime shifts can happen in minutes after news breaks.
Old data from a different information regime can mislead you.

You should still review context from prior sessions.
But your strongest signal is usually current order flow and positioning pressure.
The tape tells you who is urgent, where liquidity is thin, and how quickly price is repricing.

## Why charts are usually secondary

Charts can help with orientation.
Charts rarely explain *why* a prediction market moves.
Most moves come from new information, not recurring technical structures.

For most prediction markets, a chart is a map of past beliefs.
Your edge comes from interpreting new information faster than the market.
Your blotter and tape show how that new information is getting priced right now.

## On technical analysis in prediction markets

Valence treats technical analysis as low-value for most prediction markets.
Classic indicator sets were built for different market structures.
Prediction markets have binary payoffs, event resolution, and frequent liquidity shocks.
Those features break many technical analysis assumptions.

If a technical signal conflicts with live tape and fresh information, prioritize tape and information.
Use technical overlays only as a secondary view, never as your primary thesis.

## Your blotter is your market pulse

Your blotter is not just a ledger.
It is your live pulse of execution quality and market state.
Use it to monitor:

- Fill quality versus your expected entry.
- How quickly your orders get hit or lifted.
- Slippage during information shocks.
- Position change by market and event.

Track mark-to-market PnL off the midprice.
Midprice marking gives a cleaner read than last-trade prints in thin books.
That keeps your risk and performance view aligned with current executable conditions.
