## What basis risk means

Basis risk is the risk that two related positions do not move together as expected.
You may think you are hedged while residual risk remains.

In prediction markets, basis risk is common because contracts can differ in:

- Resolution source.
- Wording and interpretation.
- Expiry timing.
- Liquidity profile.
- Participant mix.

## Common basis risk patterns in prediction markets

### Cross-venue contract mismatch

Two venues can list similar markets with different rulebooks.
A small wording difference can create materially different payouts.

### Timing mismatch

One market may resolve earlier than another.
News can move both markets, but one can lock while the other still trades.
That can leave you exposed during the gap.

### Granularity mismatch

You may hedge a specific outcome with a broader index market.
The hedge can fail when the specific outcome diverges from the broader basket.

### Liquidity and microstructure mismatch

One leg can trade deep while the hedge leg is thin.
You can get filled on one side and miss on the other side.
That creates execution basis risk.

## How to manage basis risk

Start by writing a clear hedge mapping before you trade.
List the exact assumptions that make two legs related.
Then monitor where those assumptions can break.

Use these controls:

- Define maximum unhedged notional per basis pair.
- Set slippage limits for hedge leg execution.
- Track spread behavior between linked markets.
- Reduce size when correlation weakens.
- Prefer contracts with matching resolution logic.

## Operational checklist

Before opening a basis trade:

1. Verify both rulebooks and resolution criteria.
2. Confirm timing alignment for settlement.
3. Check live depth in both books.
4. Define exit conditions for spread widening.

During the trade:

1. Monitor realized spread volatility.
2. Watch headline risk windows.
3. Recalculate worst-case residual exposure.

After the trade:

1. Review expected versus realized hedge performance.
2. Record failure modes in your playbook.
3. Update sizing limits for similar setups.
