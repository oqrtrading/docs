## What basis risk means

Basis risk is the risk that two related positions do not move together as expected.
You may think you are hedged while residual risk remains.

In prediction markets, basis risk is common because contracts can differ in:

- Resolution source.
- Wording and interpretation.
- Expiry timing.
- Liquidity profile.
- Participant mix.

## Common basis risk patterns in prediction markets

### Cross-venue contract mismatch

Two venues can list similar markets with different rulebooks.
A small wording difference can create materially different payouts.

### Timing mismatch

One market may resolve earlier than another.
News can move both markets, but one can lock while the other still trades.
That can leave you exposed during the gap.

### Granularity mismatch

You may hedge a specific outcome with a broader index market.
The hedge can fail when the specific outcome diverges from the broader basket.

### Liquidity and microstructure mismatch

One leg can trade deep while the hedge leg is thin.
You can get filled on one side and miss on the other side.
That creates execution basis risk.

## Quantifying basis risk

This section walks through the math behind cross-venue basis exposure. The goal is to make worst-case PnL concrete before you put on a trade.

### Notation

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $p_K$ | Price you pay (or receive) per YES contract on Kalshi |
| $p_P$ | Price you pay (or receive) per YES contract on Polymarket |
| $N$ | Number of contracts (assumed equal on both legs) |

Prediction market contracts are binary: they pay \$1 if the event occurs and \$0 otherwise. The complement (NO) pays \$1 minus the YES payout.

### PnL for a single leg

For a **long YES** position entered at price $p$:

| Outcome | PnL |
|---|---|
| Resolves YES | $1 - p$ |
| Resolves NO | $-p$ |

For a **short YES** position entered at price $p$ (equivalently, long NO at $1 - p$):

| Outcome | PnL |
|---|---|
| Resolves YES | $-(1 - p)$ |
| Resolves NO | $p$ |

### Cross-venue spread trade

Suppose the same event is listed on both Kalshi and Polymarket. You observe $p_P > p_K$ and put on a spread:

- **Buy YES on Kalshi** at $p_K$ (long leg)
- **Sell YES on Polymarket** at $p_P$ (short leg)

Net cash at entry per contract: you receive $p_P - p_K > 0$.

If both venues resolve identically, the spread is risk-free:

$$
\text{PnL}_{\text{spread}} = p_P - p_K \quad \text{(regardless of outcome)}
$$

Proof for both cases:

$$
\text{Both YES:} \quad \underbrace{(1 - p_K)}_{\text{Kalshi}} + \underbrace{-(1 - p_P)}_{\text{Polymarket}} = p_P - p_K
$$

$$
\text{Both NO:} \quad \underbrace{(-p_K)}_{\text{Kalshi}} + \underbrace{p_P}_{\text{Polymarket}} = p_P - p_K
$$

### When resolutions diverge

Basis risk materializes when the two venues **do not resolve the same way**. This can happen due to different rulebooks, resolution sources, or timing. There are four possible outcomes:

| Kalshi resolves | Polymarket resolves | Kalshi leg | Polymarket leg | Net PnL per contract |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| YES | YES | $1 - p_K$ | $-(1 - p_P)$ | $p_P - p_K$ |
| NO | NO | $-p_K$ | $p_P$ | $p_P - p_K$ |
| NO | YES | $-p_K$ | $-(1 - p_P)$ | $-(1 - p_P + p_K)$ |
| YES | NO | $1 - p_K$ | $p_P$ | $1 - p_K + p_P$ |

The two agreeing outcomes lock in your edge. The two diverging outcomes are where basis risk lives.

### Worst-case loss

The catastrophic scenario is **row 3**: your long leg (Kalshi) resolves NO while your short leg (Polymarket) resolves YES. You lose on both sides simultaneously.

$$
\text{PnL}_{\text{worst}} = -p_K - (1 - p_P) = -(1 - (p_P - p_K))
$$

Scaled to $N$ contracts:

$$
\boxed{\text{Max loss} = N \cdot \bigl(1 - (p_P - p_K)\bigr)}
$$

The worst-case loss is almost the full notional ($N$ dollars) minus the edge you collected at entry. For a typical arb spread where $p_P - p_K$ is a few cents, the worst case is close to $N$.

### Numerical example

You spot the following prices for "Team X wins the championship":

| | Kalshi | Polymarket |
|---|---|---|
| YES price | \$0.52 | \$0.57 |

You buy 1,000 YES on Kalshi and sell 1,000 YES on Polymarket, collecting $\$0.05 \times 1000 = \$50$ edge.

**If both agree (YES or NO):** you keep the \$50.

**Worst case (Kalshi NO, Polymarket YES):**

$$
\text{Max loss} = 1000 \times (1 - 0.05) = \$950
$$

You lose \$520 on the Kalshi leg and \$430 on the Polymarket leg, totaling \$950. The \$50 you collected at entry partially offsets, but the net is still \$950 in the hole.

**Best case divergence (Kalshi YES, Polymarket NO):**

$$
\text{Best case} = 1000 \times (1 + 0.05) = \$1050
$$

This is the mirror scenario where you win on both legs.

### Unequal sizing

If you trade different notionals on each leg ($N_K$ on Kalshi and $N_P$ on Polymarket), the worst case becomes:

$$
\text{PnL}_{\text{worst}} = -N_K \cdot p_K - N_P \cdot (1 - p_P)
$$

And the general PnL across all four states is:

$$
\text{PnL} = N_K \cdot \text{PnL}_K(\text{outcome}_K) + N_P \cdot \text{PnL}_P(\text{outcome}_P)
$$

where each leg's PnL depends on its own venue's resolution. The legs are only correlated to the extent that the underlying events are truly the same — which is exactly the assumption that basis risk attacks.

### Implied break-even divergence probability

If you assume the spread is fair (zero expected value), you can back out the market-implied probability $q$ that the two venues diverge:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{PnL}] = (1 - q)(p_P - p_K) + q \cdot \mathbb{E}[\text{PnL} \mid \text{diverge}] = 0
$$

In the symmetric case where both divergence directions are equally likely:

$$
q = \frac{p_P - p_K}{1}
$$

A 5-cent spread implies roughly a 5% chance of resolution divergence. If you believe the true divergence probability is lower than what the spread implies, the trade has positive expected value.

## How to manage basis risk

Start by writing a clear hedge mapping before you trade.
List the exact assumptions that make two legs related.
Then monitor where those assumptions can break.

Use these controls:

- Define maximum unhedged notional per basis pair.
- Set slippage limits for hedge leg execution.
- Track spread behavior between linked markets.
- Reduce size when correlation weakens.
- Prefer contracts with matching resolution logic.

## Operational checklist

Before opening a basis trade:

1. Verify both rulebooks and resolution criteria.
2. Confirm timing alignment for settlement.
3. Check live depth in both books.
4. Define exit conditions for spread widening.

During the trade:

1. Monitor realized spread volatility.
2. Watch headline risk windows.
3. Recalculate worst-case residual exposure.

After the trade:

1. Review expected versus realized hedge performance.
2. Record failure modes in your playbook.
3. Update sizing limits for similar setups.
